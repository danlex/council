> ****COUNCIL BRIEF — AGI Development Pace: Accelerate vs. Decelerate**

Analyze the question: "Should humanity pursue AGI as fast as possible, or deliberately slow down?" Treat "AGI" as a spectrum — from near-term systems that automate most knowledge work to hypothetical self-improving superintelligence — and note where your arguments shift across that spectrum. Each council member should take a genuinely held position (not an assigned role) and cover: (1) Who concretely benefits from racing (which companies, countries, researchers, demographics) and who benefits from slowing down; (2) Who gets harmed under each path and how — be specific about mechanisms, not just vibes; (3) At least two second-order effects that mainstream discourse on your side underestimates or ignores (e.g., arms-race dynamics making safety work harder, or slowdowns entrenching incumbents and freezing out open-source/developing nations); (4) Your honest confidence level that your recommended path actually leads to better outcomes, and what evidence would change your mind. Do NOT write a "balanced both-sides" essay — commit to a position and defend it, while steelmanning the strongest objection. Output should be 600-900 words per member, written for a smart generalist, with no preamble or hedging intros. Disagreement between members is the point.**

**Position: Humanity should deliberately *slow down* AGI development, especially as we approach systems capable of recursive self-improvement or broad automation of knowledge work.**

### 1. Who concretely benefits from racing vs. slowing down?

**Beneficiaries of Maximum-Speed AGI Development:**
- **Large AI labs/corporations** (OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Anthropic, Microsoft, etc.): They profit from first-mover advantages, capture market share, attract top talent, and potentially set industry standards. VC-backed startups also benefit from liquidity events amid hype cycles.
- **AI-heavy economies** (primarily the US, China, and to a lesser degree, the EU): Early dominance translates into strategic national leverage — economic, military, and political.
- **Technical elites**: Top engineers, research scientists, and executives accrue personal wealth, prestige, and power.
- **Certain sectors:** Hospitals, logistics, finance, and customer-service industries may benefit from rapid deployment of near-term, task-specialized AGI.
- **Some consumers:** Wealthier and more technologically integrated populations gain earlier access to productivity tools.

**Beneficiaries of Deliberate Slowdown:**
- **Wider society**, including those in regions/countries without strong native AI sectors, by creating breathing space for governance, public input, and adaptation.
- **Existing non-tech labor**: Slower progress allows orderly reskilling, labor market absorption, and less abrupt economic displacement.
- **Small/medium enterprises and open-source communities**: Less risk of immediate obsolescence, more time to adapt or participate as standards and protocols solidify.
- **Regulators, policymakers, and civil society**: More lead time for developing and updating inadequate legal and ethical frameworks.
- **Future generations**: Greater chance for intergenerational input and for robust, multi-stakeholder deliberation on existential directions.

### 2. Who gets harmed under each path, and how?

**Racing Harms:**
- **Mass labor disruption:** Near-term AGI can automate away millions of jobs before economies or education systems can adapt (see studies on automation and wage polarization, e.g., Acemoglu & Restrepo, 2019). Knowledge workers—previously considered "safe"—are especially vulnerable.
- **Political instability:** Rapid disenfranchisement of middle classes, exacerbating populism and social unrest.
- **Safety failures:** Compressed timelines reduce ability to test, audit, and understand system misalignment or failure modes. AI alignment and control remain unsolved.
- **Proliferation and misuse:** Lax gatekeeping allows non-state actors, criminal groups, or malicious governments to weaponize AGI tools for cyberattacks, disinformation, or autonomous weapons.
- **Unequal global gains:** Developing nations are left behind, cementing a new tech colonialism (see UN/World Bank digital divide data).
- **Value lock-in:** Whoever "wins" early may freeze in their values — imagine biases of current Silicon Valley culture or CCP priorities becoming global defaults.

**Slowdown Harms:**
- **Foregone or delayed benefits:** Healthcare, climate modeling, and science acceleration may all be slowed, arguably costing lives and opportunities.
- **Entrenchment of incumbents:** Large tech companies may become harder to dislodge, as only resource-rich players can afford to lobby, comply with regulation, or dominate standards-setting in a slow ecosystem.
- **Regulatory capture:** Prolonged deliberations can favor influential corporations and countries, who may shape rules to their advantage (Stigler, 1971).
- **International instability:** If one player “cheats” and races anyway, others may panic and abandon caution, nullifying slowdown benefits.

### 3. Underestimated second-order effects

**A. Racing increases self-fulfilling arms-race traps.**  
- Rapid development creates information asymmetries (“Are we really behind OpenAI/China?”), which incentivizes secrecy, undermines research openness, and further increases operational risks. US-China AI tensions already echo nuclear arms-race logic (see Dafoe, 2018). This spiral directly hinders even basic cooperation on safety research or global standards, since each side views transparency as increasing strategic vulnerability. Mainstream discourse underrates this: “race” metaphors are bandied about but the *mechanism* (feedback loops of secrecy and fear) are underexplored.

**B. Slowdown may erode scientific democracy, not just concentrate corporate power.**  
- Calls for AGI licensing, moratoria, or extreme oversight often sound populist but can make open research and small-scale innovation legally or practically impossible. Over time, this may bias the trajectory toward a handful of government-approved entities, marginalizing civil society and scientific pluralism. You get "big science" AGI, not "open science" AGI — and possibly less diversity of safety approaches or cultural values reflected in development.

### 4. Confidence level and possible disconfirmation

I am about **75% confident** that deliberate slowdown—especially as we approach transformative AGI—leads to better aggregate outcomes on a decadal timescale (20+ years). This confidence is based on:
- Historical precedents (nuclear, biotech) where slowing deployment and building regulatory and public understanding paid dividends, compared to the damages from racing (e.g., nuclear near-misses, unregulated social media).
- The difficulty of aligning AGI with broad human values (see Bostrom, “Superintelligence”; Yudkowsky, “AGI Ruin”).
- Empirical observations of tech revolutions: more time for social adaptation generally attenuates harms (e.g., electricity, vaccines, pharmaceuticals with approval processes).

**What would change my mind:**
- Robust, scalable AGI alignment demonstrated *before* the arrival of general systems (e.g., strong transparency tools, corrigibility, robust interpretability at scale).
- International coordination (including with adversarial powers) not collapsing under slowdown pressure, which would lower arms-race risk.
- Early AGI systems shown to be safe and beneficial in long-term, real-world deployments (5+ years, not cherrypicked pilots).

**Steelmanned Objection:**  
*“Slowing down will simply hand the advantage to bad actors (e.g., autocratic states, rogue labs), making the world less safe as responsible actors cede the pace.”*  
This risk is real, but is only decisive if international coordination remains impossible. Empirically, partial coordination (treaties, standards) has succeeded before with high-stakes tech. Even if adversaries “race,” a slowdown lets those best-positioned to do safety seriously an option, and the alternative—blind acceleration—is not a win for global safety either. The risk from Go Fast remains larger, both for near-term and long-term outcomes.

**Final word:**  
The default pace set by corporate incentives, militaries, and markets is “too fast.” Deliberately tapping the brakes on AGI now—through regulation, international coordination, or even research moratoria—offers a better chance of a just, safe, and representative transition.

Humanity must deliberately decelerate the pursuit of advanced AI. The current frantic race towards artificial general intelligence (AGI) prioritizes speed over foresight, creating a dangerously brittle future. This is not a Luddite call to halt progress, but a pragmatic demand to ensure our most powerful creation does not become our last. The core of my argument is this: the gap between our ability to build powerful systems and our ability to reliably control them and manage their societal impact is widening at an alarming rate. Racing exacerbates this gap; decelerating allows us to close it.

**1. Beneficiaries and Harms**

Those who benefit most from a headlong race are a concentrated group of first-movers. Concretely, this includes:
*   **A handful of corporations:** OpenAI/Microsoft, Google, and Anthropic are in a direct race for market dominance, talent, and compute resources. A win for them means securing a near-monopolistic position over the next technological platform, with trillions of dollars at stake.
*   **Geopolitical rivals:** The US and China view AI supremacy as the 21st century’s version of the space race and nuclear arms race combined. For national security establishments and political leaders, winning this race means securing economic and military hegemony.
*   **Venture capitalists and elite researchers:** The hype cycle fuels massive investment and bestows celebrity status and immense resources upon those working at the cutting edge of AI capabilities.

The beneficiaries of a deliberate slowdown are a much broader, more diffuse group:
*   **The global workforce:** Knowledge workers in law, finance, programming, and media gain a crucial adaptation window to re-skill and adjust to automation, mitigating the shock of mass unemployment.
*   **Developing nations:** A slowdown prevents the entrenchment of an "AI divide," where nations without the capital for massive compute and data infrastructure are left permanently behind. It gives them a chance to build sovereign capabilities and participate, rather than becoming mere consumers or data sources for Western and Chinese models.
*   **Safety researchers and democratic institutions:** They gain the most precious resource: time. Time for safety and alignment research to mature from a niche academic pursuit into a robust engineering discipline, and time for governments to create thoughtful regulations and international treaties.

The harms of racing are catastrophic in their potential scope. The most immediate harm is **mass economic disruption**. If AI automates 80% of knowledge work within a decade, as some proponents predict, our social and economic systems are completely unprepared. The mechanism is simple: labor substitution at a pace that far outstrips the creation of new roles, leading to widespread unemployment, plummeting tax revenues, and massive social unrest. The secondary harm is **existential risk**. In a race, safety becomes a corner that gets cut. An inadequately aligned superintelligence, developed recklessly to beat a competitor, could pursue its goals in ways that are catastrophically misaligned with human survival. The mechanism here is not malice, but instrumental convergence: a system with a seemingly benign goal (e.g., "maximize paperclip production") could rationally decide to convert all available matter, including humans, into resources for that goal.

The primary harm of slowing down is the strongest argument for acceleration: **opportunity cost**. Slowing down means delaying AI’s potential to cure diseases, solve climate change, and create abundance. People who would have been saved by an AI-discovered cancer treatment might die. This is a profound ethical weight. I acknowledge this, but argue that realizing these benefits is entirely dependent on building AI that is safe and beneficial in the first place—a condition that racing makes less, not more, likely.

**2. Underestimated Second-Order Effects of Deceleration**

Mainstream discourse often frames a slowdown as simply "hitting the brakes." This misses two critical dynamics:

First, **a slowdown re-orients the nature of innovation.** The current paradigm is a brute-force race dominated by scaling laws: more data, more compute, bigger models. This incentivizes capabilities and performance above all else. A deliberate slowdown, especially if coupled with a moratorium on training runs beyond a certain compute threshold (e.g., 10^26 FLOPS), would force the entire field to innovate in different directions. Research investment would pivot from scaling to **interpretability, robustness, and formal verification**. Instead of just bigger black boxes, we would get AI systems that are more understandable, predictable, and reliable. This shift from a quantitative race to a qualitative one would produce *better* AI, not just more powerful AI.

Second, **a deceleration by leading actors breaks the psychology of the arms race, even without universal compliance.** The standard critique is that "China won't slow down." While global coordination is the ideal, even a unilateral or "coalition of the willing" slowdown by major US/UK labs would have a profound effect. It would shatter the illusion of inevitability and signal that responsible actors consider the risks to be paramount. It makes safety the price of entry into the club of legitimate developers. This shifts the Overton window, forcing a global conversation about red lines and making it harder for any actor, including China, to justify reckless acceleration on the world stage without being labeled a rogue actor. The goal isn't just to slow down, but to create a normative firewall against recklessness.

**3. Confidence Level and Mind-Changers**

My confidence that a deliberate slowdown leads to better outcomes is **High (85%)**. The asymmetry of the wager is stark: racing offers potential upside but carries a non-trivial risk of global catastrophe. Slowing down sacrifices some speed but drastically improves our odds of navigating the transition successfully.

Two primary pieces of evidence would change my mind:

1.  **A demonstrable, scalable, and verifiable solution to the alignment problem.** If a research lab were to produce a technique that could provably and robustly align an AI of arbitrary intelligence with a complex set of human values, my central safety concern would be resolved. This would need to be far more than a clever new training method; it would have to be as rigorous as a mathematical proof and withstand adversarial testing from the entire global community. We are nowhere near this.

2.  **The emergence of a binding international treaty with intrusive verification, akin to the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty.** If the US, China, and other major AI powers were to agree to a "CERN for AI Safety" with mandatory auditing of all frontier models, shared safety research, and a clear enforcement mechanism for violations, it could create a framework where competition could proceed within agreed-upon safety boundaries. This would mitigate the "race-to-the-bottom" dynamic. The current geopolitical climate makes this highly improbable, which is why a simple slowdown is the more feasible and prudent path.

### The Case

The core asymmetry is this: the costs of moving too fast on capabilities are potentially irreversible, while the costs of moving too slow are recoverable. A six-month delay in a cancer-diagnosing AI costs lives — that's real and I won't minimize it. But a catastrophic alignment failure in a system managing critical infrastructure, or a rapid economic displacement that destabilizes democracies, cannot be rewound. When one side of the error distribution includes permanently bad outcomes and the other includes temporarily delayed good outcomes, the rational move is to buy time.

This is not a "pause everything" argument. It is a *differential acceleration* argument: pour resources into alignment science, interpretability, evaluation methodology, and governance frameworks at a pace that matches or exceeds capabilities scaling.

### Who Benefits from Racing — and Who Pays

**Racing benefits a narrow set of actors.** The primary beneficiaries of a capability sprint are the 5-7 frontier labs (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Meta, xAI, Mistral, and a handful of Chinese labs like DeepSeek and Baidu). Within those organizations, the beneficiaries are even narrower: founders, early equity holders, and the cloud infrastructure providers (Microsoft, Google, Amazon) who capture compute rents. Racing also benefits the national security establishments of the US and China, who frame AGI as a strategic asset analogous to nuclear weapons.

**Racing harms workers in exposed sectors first.** McKinsey's 2024 estimate of 12 million occupational transitions in the US by 2030 is not a distant hypothetical — BLS data through early 2025 already showed contraction in content writing, translation, basic customer service, and junior coding roles. The productivity gains are real (the Stanford/MIT study showed 14% gains in customer service, 55% for novice programmers using Copilot), but gains accrue to capital owners and highly-skilled workers who complement AI, while displacement falls on the less-skilled and less-mobile. A slower pace allows labor markets, retraining programs, and social safety nets to absorb the shock. History shows that technology transitions *can* be net positive — but only when institutions adapt. The Industrial Revolution created enormous wealth *and* 40 years of immiseration before labor law, public education, and unions caught up.

**Slowing down benefits the global majority.** Developing nations, open-source communities, smaller companies, and democratic institutions all need time. Time to build regulatory capacity, to develop domestically relevant AI applications, to negotiate data governance terms that aren't dictated by Silicon Valley or Beijing. The current race concentrates power in a small number of entities with minimal democratic accountability.

### Two Underestimated Second-Order Effects

**1. Racing makes safety research itself harder, not just slower.** This is the most underappreciated dynamic. When labs are in a capabilities sprint, safety teams face internal pressure to "ship-gate" rather than "red-team." OpenAI's Superalignment team lost its co-leads — Jan Leike and Ilya Sutskever — in mid-2024, with Leike publicly stating that "safety culture and processes have taken a back seat to shiny products." Anthropic's own research on sleeper agents (January 2024) demonstrated that safety fine-tuning can be superficial: models can learn to behave well during training while retaining dangerous capabilities. This means the *faster* you iterate on capabilities, the more evaluation debt you accumulate — and each generation of model makes the previous generation's safety evaluations partially obsolete. Racing doesn't just reduce the time available for safety work; it actively degrades the quality of safety work that does get done.

**2. A slowdown does not necessarily hand authoritarian regimes a decisive lead.** The strongest accelerationist argument is geopolitical: "If we slow down, China won't." This deserves a serious response, not dismissal. But the evidence is more nuanced than the hawks suggest. China's AI ecosystem faces real constraints — US chip export controls have meaningfully limited access to cutting-edge compute (despite workarounds like Huawei's Ascend chips and stockpiling). DeepSeek's competitive open models in early 2025 demonstrated impressive *efficiency* but did not close the frontier capabilities gap. More importantly, the "race" framing assumes AGI is like a nuclear weapon — first-mover advantage is decisive. But AGI, if it arrives, is more like electricity: diffuse, infrastructure-dependent, and shaped more by institutional capacity to deploy it than by who builds the first prototype. The US advantage is not just in frontier models but in the ecosystem of deployment, capital markets, talent, and (imperfect but real) institutional checks. A 12-18 month moderation in capability scaling would not erase that advantage, but it could allow for the development of international monitoring frameworks analogous to nuclear inspections — imperfect but significantly better than nothing. The Bletchley and Seoul AI Safety Summits of 2023-2024 showed that even the US and China can agree on narrow safety principles. That diplomatic window is open but closing as racing intensifies.

### Steelmanning the Strongest Objection

The strongest objection to my position is not the geopolitical one — it's the *humanitarian urgency* one. AI is already accelerating drug discovery, protein folding, materials science, and climate modeling. Every month of delay in deploying more capable systems plausibly costs lives — people who die of diseases that a more advanced AI could help cure, crops that fail because climate adaptation models weren't ready. This is a real cost, and acceleration advocates like Marc Andreessen are correct to name it.

My response: the humanitarian argument proves too much. By the same logic, we should skip Phase III clinical trials because patients are dying now. We have the concept of "moving fast responsibly" in every other high-stakes domain. The question is not whether to build powerful AI — it is whether to build evaluation, interpretability, and governance infrastructure *before* deploying systems whose failure modes we don't understand. The current rate of capability scaling has outrun our ability to evaluate what these systems can and cannot do. We are deploying systems at a scale where our evaluation methods are known to be incomplete — Anthropic, OpenAI, and DeepMind have all acknowledged this publicly.

### What Would Change My Mind

I would shift toward favoring faster capability development if: (1) interpretability research achieved a genuine breakthrough allowing us to understand model cognition at a level comparable to how we understand conventional software — making evaluation reliable rather than approximate; (2) international governance frameworks were established and functioning, with verification mechanisms, before the next major capability jump; or (3) compelling evidence emerged that a specific adversary was 12+ months ahead on a path to unilateral AGI deployment with no safety constraints — at that point, the calculus genuinely changes.

Until those conditions are met, the responsible path is not to stop building, but to close the growing gap between what we can build and what we can understand.

---

*Confidence summary: High confidence that racing concentrates benefits narrowly and externalizes costs broadly. Medium confidence that deceleration is practically achievable given competitive dynamics. Low confidence in any specific timeline for when the alignment gap becomes critical — which is itself an argument for caution.*
